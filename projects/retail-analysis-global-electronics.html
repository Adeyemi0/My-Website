Project Overview

A retail business came to me with a catastrophic situation: revenue had collapsed by nearly 90% over 12 months, yet the leadership team couldn't pinpoint why. The raw numbers were there — sales figures, transaction logs, customer records — but nobody had connected the dots.

My task was to build a comprehensive Power BI dashboard that would not only surface what was happening, but explain why it was happening and what to do about it. This project demonstrates the core of what I do: turn messy business data into a clear, actionable story.

Bottom line: The analysis revealed that the revenue collapse was almost entirely driven by an 85% drop in repeat customer retention — customers were buying once and never returning. Fixing acquisition while ignoring retention was burning cash.

Business Problem

The business was in firefighting mode. The leadership team had tried increasing ad spend, launching promotions, and onboarding new product lines — but revenue kept declining. Without a clear diagnosis, every intervention was a guess.

The data held answers to several critical questions they couldn't answer:

  • Was the decline driven by fewer new customers, or fewer returning ones?
  • Which product categories were losing traction — and which were still healthy?
  • Was there a geographic concentration to the losses?
  • At what point in the customer journey were people disengaging?
  • Were there seasonal patterns masking or amplifying the trend?

The challenge wasn't just technical — it was diagnostic. Building the right dashboard meant first understanding which questions, if answered, would actually change what the business decided to do next.

Dataset

The analysis was built on the client's historical transactional and customer records, structured across several related tables. The data model connected:

  • Sales transactions — order dates, quantities, product IDs, and associated store IDs
  • Customer records — anonymized demographic data, acquisition dates, and geographic location
  • Product catalog — categories, subcategories, unit prices, and margins
  • Store data — location, opening date, and channel type (online vs. physical)
  • Calendar table — built in DAX to enable time intelligence across all visuals

The datasets required significant cleaning in Power Query before modeling: date formats were inconsistent across stores, several customer IDs had duplicate entries from system migrations, and the product hierarchy needed rebuilding from flat CSV exports.

Methodology

The project followed a structured diagnostic workflow — from raw data to root cause:

1

Data Profiling & Quality Assessment

Column profiling in Power Query revealed missing values, duplicate records, and date inconsistencies. All issues were documented and resolved before any modeling began.

2

Data Modeling & Relationship Design

A star schema was built with the sales fact table at the center, linked to customer, product, store, and date dimension tables. This model powers fast, reliable DAX measures.

3

DAX Measure Development

Core business metrics were defined as reusable DAX measures: total revenue, YoY growth, customer retention rate, cohort repeat purchase rate, average order value, and customer lifetime value proxies.

4

Retention Cohort Analysis

The critical step. Customers were segmented by acquisition month and tracked across subsequent periods. This revealed that cohorts acquired in recent periods had dramatically lower return rates — the core finding.

5

Dashboard Design & Storytelling

Visuals were arranged to walk stakeholders through the story: overall trend → category breakdown → customer behaviour → root cause → recommendations. The dashboard is designed to answer questions, not just display data.

Key Insights

The analysis surfaced four findings that directly shaped the recovery strategy:

📉

Retention Was the Real Problem

An 85% drop in repeat customer rate was the primary driver of revenue decline — not acquisition. The business was refilling a leaking bucket.

📦

Category Performance Was Uneven

Two product categories were still growing while others collapsed. Resources had not been reallocated to reflect this — presenting an immediate optimization opportunity.

🗓️

Cohort Decay Accelerated Post-2023

Customers acquired before 2023 showed healthy return rates. Post-2023 cohorts showed near-zero repeat purchases — suggesting a product, fulfilment, or experience change drove the shift.

🌍

Geographic Losses Were Concentrated

Three regions accounted for over 70% of the revenue decline. Targeted regional interventions could stabilize the business faster than a broad-based approach.

Business Impact & Recommendations

The dashboard shifted the conversation from "why is revenue down?" to "here's exactly what to fix and in what order." Specific recommendations delivered:

Immediate Actions (0–30 days)

  • Launch a win-back campaign targeting the 2023–2024 cohorts who purchased once but never returned
  • Investigate what changed in the post-purchase experience after mid-2023 (delivery times, product quality, communication)
  • Reallocate marketing budget toward the two growing product categories

Medium-term (30–90 days)

  • Implement a post-purchase retention sequence (email + SMS) triggered at 14, 30, and 60-day intervals
  • Build a repeat purchase rate KPI into weekly leadership reporting to track recovery
  • Concentrate regional sales efforts on the three high-decline geographies

Structural Changes

  • Shift CAC (customer acquisition cost) measurement to include LTV projections — current CAC calculations were ignoring repeat purchase value entirely
  • Set up automated cohort reporting in Power BI for ongoing monitoring

Key principle: Increasing acquisition spend while retention was broken would have accelerated the decline, not reversed it. Data made this visible in a way that gut feel couldn't.
